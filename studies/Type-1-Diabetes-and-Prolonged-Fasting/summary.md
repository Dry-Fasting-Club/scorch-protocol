# Study Summary: Type 1 diabetes and prolonged fasting

**Topic:** Safety and insulin-adjustment guidelines for Type 1 Diabetics undertaking a 25-hour absolute dry/wet fast (Yom Kippur).
**Key Focus:** Basal reduction and "Stop Signals" for insulin-dependent fasters.

## Overview
This study followed 56 Type 1 Diabetics (ages 12-40) who attempted a 25-hour religious fast (Yom Kippur). It establishes the medical protocol for how an insulin-dependent person can safely enter a fasting state without risking Ketoacidosis (DKA) or Hypoglycemia.

## Main Findings
- **High Success Rate:** 65% of participants successfully completed the full 25-hour fast.
- **Insulin Reduction Protocol:** Successful fasters reduced their total daily insulin by **50-75%**. Basal rates were reduced by ~50%, and meal boluses were obviously eliminated.
- **Hypoglycemia Safety:** Those with better glycemic control (lower HbA1c) were actually *more* likely to experience hypoglycemia because they had less "buffer." The study suggests permitting slightly higher glucose targets during the fast for safety.
- **No DKA:** Zero episodes of Diabetic Ketoacidosis occurred, proving that with basal background insulin, the absence of food does not trigger the dangerous runaway ketone production seen in unmedicated T1D.
- **Stop Signals:** The fast was terminated if glucose dropped below 4.4 mmol/L (80 mg/dL) or rose above 19.4 mmol/L (350 mg/dL).

## Conclusion
Prolonged fasting (>25 hours) is feasible and safe for Type 1 Diabetics, provided they aggressively reduce their basal insulin (by at least 50%) and monitor glucose every 2.5 hours. It demystifies the idea that T1Ds "cannot fast."
