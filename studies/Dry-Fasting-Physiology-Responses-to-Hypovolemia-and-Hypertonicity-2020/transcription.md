# Dry Fasting Physiology: Responses to Hypovolemia and Hypertonicity

**Authors:** Ioannis-Eleemon Papagiannopoulos-Vatopaidinos, Maria Papagiannopoulou, Vassilis Sideris
**Published in:** *Complementary Medicine Research* (2020)

---

## Abstract

**Objective:** The aim of this study was to provide a deeper insight into dry fasting (DF) physiology. **Design:** Ten participants performed DF for 5 consecutive days. **Methods:** The following parameters were monitored daily: cortisol, aldosterone, high-sensitivity C-reactive protein (CRP), erythropoietin, albumin, uric acid, and vitamin C in serum; vasopressin (ADH), adrenocorticotropic hormone (ACTH), renin, angiotensin II, and total antioxidant capacity (TAC) in plasma; hematocrit and erythrocytes in whole blood; osmolality, noradrenaline, dopamine, adrenaline, Na+, and K+ in 24-h urine; waist circumference and body, urine, and stool weight. **Results:** The following parameters increased: ADH (60 ± 11%), ACTH (176 ± 34%), cortisol (495 ± 75%), urine osmolality (20 ± 4%), CRP (167 ± 77%), renin (315 ± 63%), angiotensin II (74 ± 21%), aldosterone (61 ± 21%), TAC (80.4 ± 17%), uric acid (103 ± 19%), albumin (18.4 ± 2.4%), erythrocytes (13.4 ± 2.2%), hematocrit (11 ± 1.8%), and the excretion of noradrenaline (40.3 ± 10%) and dopamine (17 ± 5%). The following parameters decreased: waist circumference (8.20 ± 0.61 cm), body weight (7.010 ± 0.3 kg), erythropoietin (65 ± 18%), and the excretion of adrenaline (38 ± 4%) and Na+ (60 ± 16%). The excretion of K+ remained unchanged. Vitamin C decreased, showing a half-life of 4.8 ± 0.7 days. The percent ratios of lost weight components were: urine (52.2 ± 3.7%), insensible water loss (32.2 ± 1.4%), stool (5 ± 0.3%), and respiratory gases, i.e., expired CO2 – incorporated O2 (10.6 ± 5.4%). **Conclusion:** The mechanisms underlying the hypertonicity and hypovolemia compensation and the ratio analysis of lost weight components were presented. DF demonstrated short-term antioxidant, anti-ischemic, immune-stimulating, anti-edematous, and anti-inflammatory effects. The results may have an impact on developing new concepts for the treatment of edema, obesity, and inflammatory and ischemic diseases.

---

## Introduction

Dry fasting (DF) is any type of fasting where both food and water intake are restricted. It is widely applied for religious reasons, as in Ramadan or for Great Lent, but it is also used for therapeutic purposes in some cultures. While therapeutic calorie restriction (water fasting) has been well investigated and holds a significant place in medicine, very little is known about DF. Therapeutic DF usually takes place for 1–5 days. During DF, the body is under a double pressure, i.e., the absence of food and water. This pressure leads to a unique physiological state where the body must defend against both hypertonicity and hypovolemia.

The aim of this study was to monitor a broad set of parameters during 5 days of DF to understand how the body responds to these stressors and to investigate potential therapeutic effects.

---

## Subjects and Methods

### Subjects and Design

Ten healthy adult volunteers (5 males and 5 females, age 32 ± 11 years, weight 74 ± 10 kg, body mass index 24.2 ± 3.1) participated in the study. All participants were familiar with DF as they had performed it for religious reasons in the past. The study was conducted during the 40-day pre-Easter fast (Great Lent). The participants performed total food and water restriction for 5 consecutive days. They were instructed to avoid any strenuous physical activity and stayed in a temperature-controlled environment (21–23°C).

### Hormonal, Biochemical, and Hematological Measurements

Blood samples were collected daily at 8:00 a.m. after the participants had rested for 15 min. Serum and plasma were separated and stored at –80°C until analysis. The following parameters were measured:
- **Serum:** Cortisol (chemiluminescence immunoassay), aldosterone (radioimmunoassay), high-sensitivity CRP (immunonephelometry), erythropoietin (chemiluminescence immunoassay), albumin (photometry), uric acid (photometry), and vitamin C (high-performance liquid chromatography).
- **Plasma:** ADH (radioimmunoassay), ACTH (chemiluminescence immunoassay), renin (chemiluminescence immunoassay), angiotensin II (radioimmunoassay), and TAC (photometry).
- **Whole Blood:** Hematocrit and erythrocytes (automated hematology analyzer).
- **24-h Urine:** Osmolality (freezing point depression), Na+, and K+ (indirect potentiometry), and noradrenaline, dopamine, and adrenaline (high-performance liquid chromatography).

### Somatometric Measurements

Body weight and stool weight were measured daily using a calibrated electronic scale (accuracy ±10 g). Urine weight was measured with a calibrated electronic scale (accuracy ±1 g). Waist circumference was measured daily at the midpoint between the lower costal margin and the iliac crest.

### Statistical Analysis

Data are presented as mean ± standard deviation. Statistical significance was determined using the Wilcoxon signed-rank test to compare values from day 0 to day 5. p < 0.05 was considered statistically significant.

## Results

### Body Weight, Waist Circumference, and Urine Discharge

The body weight decreased linearly by 7.010 ± 0.3 kg (9.5 ± 0.4%) after 5 days of DF (Table 1; Fig. 1). The waist circumference decreased by 8.20 ± 0.61 cm (9.3 ± 0.7%). The urine discharge decreased by 60 ± 16% over the 5 days. Despite the total restriction of water intake, urine production continued throughout the fast, indicating the mobilization of metabolic water.

### Hormonal and Biochemical Changes

The results of the hormonal and biochemical measurements are summarized in Table 1.
- **Hypovolemia and Hypertonicity Defense:** Significant increases were observed in ADH (60 ± 11%), ACTH (176 ± 34%), cortisol (495 ± 75%), renin (315 ± 63%), angiotensin II (74 ± 21%), and aldosterone (61 ± 21%). Urine osmolality increased by 20 ± 4%, reaching a maximum of 1,220 ± 55 mOsm/kg.
- **Oxidative Stress and Inflammation:** TAC increased by 80.4 ± 17% and uric acid by 103 ± 19%. Serum CRP increased significantly by 167 ± 77%. Serum vitamin C decreased with a half-life of 4.8 ± 0.7 days.
- **Blood Parameters:** Erythrocytes increased by 13.4 ± 2.2%, hematocrit by 11 ± 1.8%, and albumin by 18.4 ± 2.4%, reflecting a decrease in plasma volume.

---

## Discussion

### Hypovolemia and Hypertonicity: Sensing and Defending

During DF, the body is forced to defend its fluid homeostasis without any external intake. The increase in plasma osmolality (hypertonicity) and the decrease in plasma volume (hypovolemia) are the primary stressors. The body responds by activating the ADH and RAAS systems. The 60% increase in ADH ensures maximum water reabsorption in the kidneys, as evidenced by the high urine osmolality (1,220 mOsm/kg). The activation of the RAAS (renin, angiotensin II, aldosterone) leads to a 60% reduction in Na+ excretion, helping to maintain osmotic pressure and circulating volume.

### Ratio Analysis of Lost Weight Components

The linear weight loss of 7 kg over 5 days was composed of:
- **Urine (52.2%):** Even with maximum ADH effect, a minimum urine volume is required for waste excretion.
- **Insensible Water Loss (32.2%):** This includes water lost through sweat and the skin.
- **Stool (5%):** Minimal loss through fecal matter.
- **Respiratory Gases (10.6%):** This represents the weight difference between expired CO2 and incorporated O2, primarily from the oxidation of fat. This confirms that a significant portion of the water required by the body is provided by metabolic water produced during fat burning.

### Anti-Inflammatory and Antioxidant Effects

One of the most interesting findings was the 167% increase in CRP and the 80% increase in TAC. This suggests that DF triggers an acute-phase response that stimulates the immune system and enhances the body's antioxidant defenses. The increase in uric acid, a potent antioxidant, also contributes to this effect. These findings provide a physiological basis for the therapeutic use of DF in inflammatory and ischemic diseases.

### Summary, Limits, and Future Perspectives

DF is a powerful physiological intervention. The body demonstrates a remarkable ability to maintain homeostasis through complex hormonal and metabolic adaptations. However, the decrease in vitamin C and the intense pressure on the renal and endocrine systems suggest that multi-day DF should only be performed by healthy individuals and under medical supervision. Future research should investigate the long-term effects of DF and its application in specific clinical conditions.

---

## Conclusion

The study concludes that dry fasting demonstrates short-term antioxidant, anti-ischemic, immune-stimulating, anti-edematous, and anti-inflammatory effects. The mechanisms underlying the hypertonicity and hypovolemia compensation were presented, and the ratio analysis of lost weight components confirmed the role of metabolic water. These findings may have an impact on developing new concepts for the treatment of edema, obesity, and inflammatory and ischemic diseases.
