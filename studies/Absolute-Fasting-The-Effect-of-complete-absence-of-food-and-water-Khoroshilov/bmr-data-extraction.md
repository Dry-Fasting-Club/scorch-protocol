# BMR and Metabolic Tracking: Absolute Fasting (Khoroshilov Study)

## Overview
This study provides a broad analysis of how the human body adapts to the total absence of external food and water. It establishes the baseline for "endogenous nutrition" and quantifies the efficiency of metabolic water production compared to traditional wet fasting. This foundational work demonstrates that humans possess sophisticated evolutionary adaptations for water scarcity.

## Study Design & Methodology

### Participants
- **Total Sample:** 86 subjects
- **Age Range:** 22-58 years
- **Study Groups:**
  - Group A: 3-day dry fast (n=62)
  - Group B: 7-day wet fast comparison (n=24)
- **Conditions:** Temperature-controlled environment (20-22°C, 40-60% humidity)

This document extracts and analyzes the metabolic data from I.E. Khoroshilov's study, focusing on energy expenditure, basal metabolic rate (BMR), and the body's internal water production.

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **The Day 3 Correlation:** Both studies confirm that the most significant metabolic shift happens by **Day 3**. In Khoroshilov's study, active T3 (the primary driver of BMR) has already dropped by 25%, matching the 14-15% reduction in measured energy expenditure. But we're ignoring TSH, it drops significantly more from -25% at 3 days, to -74% at 5 days. This shows that the HPT axis is the primary driver of metabolic conservation, while the HPA axis is secondary. This also shows that the longer fasts can lead to deeper slowdown that is not easily reversed after the fast. Since carbohydrates are the primary driver of thyroid hormone production, refeeding after such a fast in a ketogenic state can lead to permanent damage to the thyroid system, including hypothyroidism and tissue-level hormone resistance.

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **TSH going down 75% but T4 levels drop only 19%:** This shows that T4 levels are not following TSH, so blood levels must be fairly normal otherwise. T3 drops by around 25% at both 3 days and 5 days so we can assume T3 levels stabilize around here. But since TSH is still dropping aggressively, it indicated the cells are not uptaking the T3 as much, because there's a mismatch to TSH. Now pair that with a low carb refeed to stay in ketosis and you keep putting adaptive pressure to keep the TSH low. This is why many who over fast, over diet, may have an altered artificially low TSH which makes blood tests seem normal, but they are not. Also genetics play a role, with most people being able to rebound within days to weeks, but if youre genetically predisposed to bad thyroid conversion, this adds extra negative pressure to the thyroid system.

### Key Insight: The "Stress Spike" and Thyroid Override
The initial 8% increase in BMR during Day 1 of a dry fast is a survival mechanism. The body ramps up energy production to facilitate the rapid switch from glucose to fat burning. This is why many people feel a "burst" of energy or restlessness in the first 24 hours. 

However, the -15% drop by Day 3 is a critical warning sign. While the HPA axis (stress response) is still rising, the total energy drop is effectively **23%** from the Day 1 peak. This proves that the **T3 impact (HPT axis)** is significantly stronger than the HPA axis impact. It shows an inverse correlation: as stress chemicals rise, thyroid hormone levels at the tissue level are forced down to prevent the body from "burning out" its water stores. While ketosis is powerful for healing especially for autophagy and growth hormone production, staying in this "metabolic brake" state too long can lead to permanent damage to the thyroid system, including hypothyroidism and tissue-level hormone resistance.

## 2. Weight Loss Economics: Dry vs. Wet
**Methodology & Intent:** Researchers used **high-precision daily weighing** combined with **Bioimpedance Analysis (BIA)**. This allowed them to separate simple dehydration (water loss) from actual tissue catabolism (fat and muscle loss).

The study explains why dry fasting results in significantly more weight loss than wet fasting.

- **Dry Fasting:** 1.1 ± 0.15 kg/day
- **Wet Fasting:** 0.5 ± 0.08 kg/day
- **Efficiency Ratio:** 2.2x faster weight loss.

### Insight: The Fat-Water Connection
Why is the loss 2.2x higher? In wet fasting, the body burns fat solely for energy. In dry fasting, the body burns fat for **energy AND water**. Because the body must "incinerate" fat to unlock the hydrogen needed to make metabolic water, the rate of fat oxidation increases dramatically.

## 3. Metabolic Water Math (The "Internal Reservoir")
**Methodology & Intent:** This data was not just "guessed"—it was derived by linking **calorimetry data** (fuel burning rates) with **nitrogen excretion levels** (protein burning). By knowing exactly how much fat and protein were oxidized, researchers could calculate the precise volume of water released at the mitochondrial level.

The study quantifies the efficiency of the body as a water-producing plant.

- **Total Production:** ~650 mL/day (Range: 500 - 800 mL)
- **Primary Source:** Fat oxidation (100g fat → 107g water)
- **Fuel Distribution (Day 3):** Fat accounts for **82%** of all energy production.

### Insight: Protein Sparing
A common question is: "Does the body burn muscle for water?"
**The Data says NO.** The study found that protein loss was only 6% of the total weight loss, with 97-99% of lean tissue being preserved. The body selectively targets adipose tissue because it is the most efficient source of both energy and water.

## 4. Conservation Mechanisms (The Defense Shield)
**Methodology & Intent:** To understand how the body "holds its breath" for water, Khoroshilov used **hormonal blood assays** to track T3/T4 levels, **gravimetric perspiration sensors** to measure sweat rate, and **core temperature monitoring**.

By Day 3, the body enters a deep protective state to prevent dehydration.

- **Thyroid Adjustment:** T3 levels drop by **25%** to slow down the engine.
- **Fluid Conservation:** Perspiration (sweating) is reduced by **60%**.
- **Temperature Control:** Core temperature set-point is lowered by ~0.4°C.
- **Skin Dynamics:** Vasoconstriction reduces core-to-skin blood flow to keep moisture locked in.


## 6. Potential Questions & Clinical Insights

### Q: Is the 15% drop in BMR a sign of "starvation mode"?
**A:** No. In the context of the Khoroshilov study, this is "Metabolic Hygiene." It is a controlled, adaptive reduction to ensure survival. Unlike long-term calorie restriction which can damage the metabolism, this is a 72-hour reset that ends with a surge in Growth Hormone (+250%) during refeeding to rebuild.

### Q: Why does heart rate increase if BMR decreases?
**A:** Heart rate increases (+15%) not because of a higher metabolic demand, but because blood volume decreases (-16% hemoconcentration). The heart beats slightly faster to maintain blood pressure and oxygen delivery with less total fluid.

### Q: Does this data support "Metabolic Tracking" for users?
**A:** Yes. Tracking resting heart rate (RHR) and body temperature can help identify when the body has shifted from the Day 1 "Stress" phase to the Day 3 "Conservation" phase. A drop in temperature of 0.4°C is a clear indicator that deep fat oxidation and metabolic water production are at their peak.

## 6. Presentation Points for Clinicians

- **The 340% Autophagy Surge:** The metabolic demand for water forces the body to recycle damaged proteins and "stagnant" site fluids at nearly triple the rate of wet fasting.
- **Evolutionary Advantage:** Humans are "fat-to-water" specialists. This study proves that a 3-day dry fast does not lead to dangerous dehydration but rather a sophisticated internal hydration shift.
- **Cardiovascular Rest:** Despite the slight HR increase, the reduction in total blood volume leads to a significant reduction in blood pressure (-30 mmHg systolic), providing a "rest" for the arterial system.
- **Time Compression:** The study confirms that 3 days of dry fasting achieves the same metabolic and ketone milestones as 7-9 days of traditional wet fasting.
